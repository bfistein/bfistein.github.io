Wedding Themes

Here are some of the Jewish terms and concepts you might want to know.

And all the crazy Ukrainian stuff you will see.

  • Kippah

    AKA Jarmulka/Yamaka

    Literally means "dome" in Hebrew. A small cap to be worn by men to cover their heads. It's supposed to remind them that "someone" is always above, watching every move they make. We will provide you with a Kippah at the ceremony.

  • Chuppah

    AKA the canopy

    The four pillars under which the bride and groom get married. It's generally covered by cloth and is tradditionally held by the groomsmen. It symbolizes the new home the couple will build together.

  • Ketubah

    AKA the pre-nup

    The ketubah is the traditional marriage contract to be signed by two witnesses. It outlines the rights and responsibilities of the groom in relation to the bride.

  • Hakafot

    AKA Circling

    When the bride reaches the Chuppah, she walks in a circle around the groom 3 times and vice versa. It's most often seen as creating a “wall of protection” around each other.

  • Sheva Brachot

    AKA the seven blessings

    Seven blessings recited by the Rabbi during the wedding ceremony over a cup of wine during the latter part of the proceedings.

  • Breaking glass

    AKA let's risk injury

    In the final moments of the ceremony, the groom steps on a glass (and hopefully breaks it without getting injured). It's said to refer to the destruction of the Temple of Jerusalem or to remind you of the fragility of marriage. After the glass is broken, all the guests yell "Mazel Tov!".

  • Mazel Tov!

    AKA Congratulations

    You should really know this one. Even the Black Eyed Peas sing about it.

  • Klezmer

    AKA the music

    Klezmer is the traditional Jewish genre of music you will get to hear, usually involving a clarinet, violin or accordion.

  • Hora

    AKA The crazy wedding dance

    At some point during the post-wedding dancing, the guests form a circle while joyful music is playing, as the bride and groom are hoisted in the air on chairs.

  • Wedding candle lighting

    AKA Ukrainianwifefinder.com

    Members of both the bride's and the groom's families' light two candles as yet another way of giving their blessings, and passing down the 'family hearth'. It was believed that as long as these candles were kept in the house the couple would have a happy life together.

  • Cutting the strings

    AKA Throwing the Bouquet, but we can't trust Angie to throw

    The bottom of the bouquet is tied to strings - one for each single girl at the wedding. The girls form a circle around the bride and each grab a string so that the bouquet is lifted in the air. Blindfolded, the bride starts to cut the strings one by one (hopefully she won't cut anything else) and whoever ends up holding the last uncut string gets to keep the bouquet.

  • Binding

    AKA Putting on the shackles

    Traditionally, the couple would get their hands tied together as a symbol of the bond created. Alternatively, utensils or other things might be bound to the newlyweds' table.

  • Rushnyk

    AKA The ritual cloth

    A traditional piece of cloth embroidered with symbols. Its diamond-shaped design is an ancient agricultural symbol, which means a sown field, or the sun, and expresses the idea of fertility and protection against evil. Ducks in the centre of the Rushnyk symbolize the element of life-giving water or a bride and a groom entering family life.

  • Rugelach, Hamantaschen & Korovai

    AKA Jewish & Ukrainian sweets

    You'll be able to sample some traditional sweets, like Rugelach (rohlíčky), Hamantaschen or Korovai. Korovai is a festive bread of round shape. The newlyweds get to eat first and they should bite into the Korovai without using their hands. The one who gets the bigger piece will wear the pants in the family.
