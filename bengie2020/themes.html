Wedding Themes

Here are some of the Jewish terms and concepts you might want know

And all the crazy Ukrainian stuff you will see.

  • Klezmer

    AKA the music

    Klezmer is the traditional Jewish genre of music you will get to hear, usually involving a clarinet, violin or accordion.

  • Kippah

    AKA Jarmulka/Yamaka

    Literally means "dome" in Hebrew. A small cap to be worn by men to cover their heads. It's supposed to remind them that "someone" is always above, watching every move they make.

  • Chuppah

    AKA the canopy

    The four pillars under which the bride and groom get married. It's generally covered by cloth and is tradditionally held by the groomsmen. It symbolizes the new home the couple will build together.

  • Breaking glass

    AKA let's risk injury

    In the final moments of the ceremony, the groom steps on a glass (and hopefully breaks it without getting injured). It's said to refer to the destruction of the Temple of Jerusalem or to remind you of the fragility of marriage. After the glass is broken, all the guests yell "Mazel Tov!".

  • Mazel Tov!

    AKA Congratulations

    You should really know this one. Even the Black Eyed Peas sing about it.

  • Ketubah

    AKA the pre-nup

    The ketubah is the traditional marriage contract to be signed by two witnesses. It outlines the rights and responsibilities of the groom in relation to the bride.

  • Hora

    AKA The crazy wedding dance

    At some point during the post-wedding dancing, the guests form a circle while joyful music is playing, as the bride and groom are hoisted in the air on chairs.

  • Sheva Brachot

    AKA the seven blessings

    Seven blessings recited by the Rabbi during the wedding ceremony over a cup of wine during the latter part of the proceedings.

  • Yichud

    AKA Seclusion

    Immediately after the wedding ceremony, the newlyweds are secluded from their guests for a brief period of time. At one time, this was where the marriage was consummated, but nowadays, the couple will simply enjoy some quiet time (and food!) before greeting their loved ones at the reception.

  • Hakafot

    AKA Circling

    When the bride reaches the Chuppah, she walks in a circle around the groom 3 times and vice versa. It's most often seen as creating a “wall of protection” around each other.

  • Kupala Night

    AKA Ukrainian craziness

    Kupala Night (July 6 to 7) has been the time of wild magic in Slavic folklore and ancient celebration dedicated to the summer solistice. Kupalo was believed to be the god of love and of the harvest and the personification of the earth’s fertility. It is believed that on this day rituals protect homes and beloved ones, strengthen health and bring happiness.

  • Kupala Night Traditions

    AKA More Ukrainian traditions

    Magical properties were ascribed to the flowers gathered that day. It was believed that such herbs could protect one from the evil forces of nature and even cure illnesses. Women adorned their hair with garlands and wreaths of freshly cut flowers to attract their soulmate. Later in the evening, they would decorate these wreaths with lit candles and float them on the water, making a wish.

  • Binding

    AKA Putting on the shackles

    Traditionally, the couple would get their hands tied together as a symbol of the bond created. Alternatively, utensils or other things might be bound to the newlyweds' table.

  • Rushnyk

    AKA The ritual cloth

    A traditional piece of cloth embroidered with symbols. Its diamond-shaped design is an ancient agricultural symbol, which means a sown field, or the sun, and expresses the idea of fertility and protection against evil. Ducks in the centre of the Rushnyk symbolize the element of life-giving water or a bride and a groom entering family life. You will see several of them on our tables.

  • Rugelach, Hamantaschen & Korovai

    AKA Jewish & Ukrainian sweets

    You'll be able to sample some traditional sweets, like Rugelach (rohlíčky), Hamantaschen or Korovai.
